ATRIAL FIBRILLATION IN POST-CARDIAC SURGERY PATIENTS — Detection & Management

EPIDEMIOLOGY
Post-operative atrial fibrillation (POAF) is the most common complication following cardiac surgery:
- CABG: 20-40% incidence
- Valve surgery: 40-60% incidence
- Combined CABG + valve: up to 65% incidence
Peak onset: 48-72 hours post-operatively (range: Day 1 to Day 10)

DETECTION CRITERIA
ECG Findings:
- Irregularly irregular RR intervals (no two consecutive beats the same)
- Absence of discrete P waves; replaced by fibrillatory baseline
- Variable ventricular rate (typically 100-180 bpm if uncontrolled)
- Narrow QRS unless aberrant conduction or bundle branch block present

Remote Monitoring Indicators:
- Heart Rate Variability (HRV) dropping acutely below 15ms (from baseline)
- Sudden heart rate increase to 110-160 bpm with irregular pattern
- AFib risk score (ML-derived) >70% warrants immediate ECG confirmation
- Patient-reported symptoms: palpitations, "heart fluttering," sudden dyspnea

CLASSIFICATION:
- Paroxysmal AF: Episodes <7 days, terminate spontaneously
- Persistent AF: >7 days, requires cardioversion
- Long-standing Persistent: >1 year
- Permanent: Cardioversion not planned/possible

MANAGEMENT PROTOCOL FOR POAF

Rate Control (First-line for hemodynamically stable):
- Metoprolol IV 2.5-5mg q5min (max 15mg) → oral transition
- Target resting heart rate: 60-100 bpm
- Digoxin may be added for rate control in HF with reduced EF

Rhythm Control:
- Amiodarone: Loading 150mg IV over 10 min, then 1mg/min × 6h, then 0.5mg/min × 18h
- Electrical cardioversion if hemodynamically unstable (DC cardioversion 200J biphasic)

Anticoagulation:
- If AF persists >48 hours: anticoagulation required to prevent thromboembolism
- CHA₂DS₂-VASc score ≥2: anticoagulate (warfarin, apixaban, rivaroxaban)
- TEE-guided cardioversion if anticoagulation status uncertain

THROMBOEMBOLIC RISK:
- POAF increases stroke risk 2-3× compared to non-AF post-surgical patients
- Left atrial thrombus can form within 24-48 hours of sustained AF
- Bridge therapy with heparin required until therapeutic anticoagulation achieved

WHEN TO ESCALATE (IMMEDIATE PHYSICIAN REVIEW REQUIRED):
- HR >150 bpm with AF pattern
- Hemodynamic instability (systolic BP <90mmHg)
- New neurological symptoms (TIA/stroke concern)
- SpO₂ <90% in setting of rapid AF
- AF persisting >6 hours despite rate control

PATIENT EDUCATION KEY POINTS:
- Irregular heartbeat sensation is common post-surgery — report immediately
- Do not stop anticoagulation without physician guidance
- Know the difference between expected recovery fatigue and palpitation symptoms
- Emergency signs: fainting, severe shortness of breath, chest pain with rapid heartbeat → call 911
