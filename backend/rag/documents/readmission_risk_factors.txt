CARDIAC SURGERY READMISSION RISK FACTORS — Evidence-Based Clinical Reference

30-DAY READMISSION RATES: CONTEXT
- National average 30-day readmission rate post-cardiac surgery: 12-17%
- Leading causes: Heart failure decompensation (31%), infection (18%), arrhythmia (15%), respiratory (12%)
- Estimated cost per readmission: $15,000-$25,000
- CMS penalizes hospitals with excess readmissions under HRRP (Hospital Readmissions Reduction Program)

HIGH-RISK FACTOR IDENTIFICATION

CARDIAC RISK FACTORS:
Reduced Ejection Fraction (EF <35%):
- 2.8× increased readmission risk
- Mechanism: Reduced cardiac reserve; minimal tolerance for volume changes, arrhythmia, infection
- Management: Strict fluid monitoring, sodium restriction, daily weights, HF specialist follow-up

Prior Heart Failure Hospitalization:
- 3.2× increased readmission risk
- Each prior HF admission increases subsequent admission probability by 40%
- Management: Specialized HF clinic transition program; nurse-led phone follow-up within 72 hours

Atrial Fibrillation History:
- 1.8× increased readmission risk
- Post-surgical AF that reverts may recur; persistent AF risk of stroke/embolism
- Management: Anticoagulation protocols, heart rate monitoring, rhythm surveillance

METABOLIC RISK FACTORS:
Type 2 Diabetes:
- 1.6× increased readmission risk
- Mechanisms: Impaired wound healing, immune dysfunction, hyperglycemia-induced inflammation, autonomic neuropathy
- Management: Perioperative glycemic control (target 140-180 mg/dL inpatient); HbA1c optimization outpatient
- Monitoring: Daily glucose checks if on insulin; foot exam at each visit

Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD):
- Stage 3 (GFR 30-59): 1.9× readmission risk
- Stage 4 (GFR 15-29): 3.1× readmission risk
- Mechanisms: Fluid retention impairs HF management; ACE inhibitor dosing limited; contrast nephropathy risk
- Monitoring: BMP weekly for 4 weeks post-discharge; watch for rising creatinine (>25% baseline)

Hypertension with End-Organ Damage:
- Poorly controlled HTN elevates afterload → graft stress, HF decompensation
- Target: BP <130/80 in first 30 days post-CABG

SOCIAL/BEHAVIORAL RISK FACTORS:
Living Alone / Limited Social Support:
- 2.4× readmission risk (strongest social risk factor)
- Mechanism: Delayed symptom recognition; inability to manage medications independently; falls
- Intervention: Check-in call within 48 hours of discharge; home health referral; emergency contact engagement

Medication Non-Adherence:
- 1.9× readmission risk; accounts for ~10% of preventable readmissions
- Risk factors for non-adherence: polypharmacy (>5 medications), cost barriers, cognitive impairment, complex schedules
- Intervention: Pill organizers, medication reconciliation, pharmacy counseling at discharge

Low Health Literacy:
- Patients with low literacy are 2× more likely to be readmitted
- Cannot interpret symptom instructions; misunderstands medication labels
- Intervention: Teach-back method at discharge; picture-based instructions; Cora-style plain-English AI companion

Substance Use (Tobacco, Alcohol):
- Active smoking: 1.8× readmission risk post-CABG (graft patency reduced 30% in smokers)
- Alcohol misuse: Risk of arrhythmia, medication non-adherence, falls

PHYSIOLOGIC EARLY WARNING INDICATORS (Remote Monitoring Thresholds):

HIGH SENSITIVITY TRIGGERS (recommend same-day outreach if 2+ present):
- Resting HR >95 bpm for >12 hours
- SpO₂ <94% for >4 hours
- HRV <20ms sustained
- Weight gain >2 lbs in 24 hours (if monitored)
- Activity <500 steps/day × 2 consecutive days
- Sleep quality <40% for 3 consecutive nights

CRITICAL TRIGGERS (immediate physician escalation):
- HR >130 bpm or irregular rhythm pattern consistent with AF
- SpO₂ <90%
- Patient-reported chest pain or severe dyspnea
- Syncope or near-syncope
- Wound dehiscence or purulent drainage

INTERVENTIONS SHOWN TO REDUCE READMISSION:
1. Transitional care nurse call within 72 hours: 20-30% readmission reduction
2. Cardiac rehabilitation enrollment: 30-50% reduction in 30-day readmission
3. Remote physiologic monitoring (wearable + AI alert): 25-38% reduction (emerging evidence)
4. Patient-reported outcomes tools: 15% reduction when actioned promptly
5. Medication reconciliation at discharge: 14% reduction in medication errors
6. Early outpatient follow-up (within 7 days): 18% reduction vs. 14-day follow-up
