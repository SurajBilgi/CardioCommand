# CardioCommand — Hackathon Presentation Script

Use this document to practice your demo and narrative. Adjust timing to your slot.

---

## Opening (≈30 seconds)

> Every year, over **800,000 open-heart surgeries** are performed in the United States alone. The first 30 days after discharge is the most dangerous window in a patient's recovery — it's when complications spike, readmissions happen, and patients feel the most scared and alone.
>
> Yet in that entire 30-day window, a patient might have only **one or two check-in calls** with their care team.
>
> That's the gap we built **CardioCommand** to close.

---

## The problem (≈60 seconds)

> Let me paint you a picture.
>
> John Mercer just had a triple bypass. He's 67 years old. He's sent home with a packet of papers, a list of medications, and a follow-up appointment in two weeks. But at 2 AM on day 5, his heart rate spikes to 118. His oxygen drops. He doesn't know if that's normal. He doesn't want to bother the on-call doctor. So he waits.
>
> Meanwhile, his cardiologist, Dr. Rao, is managing twelve post-op patients across two facilities. She has no visibility into what's happening to any of them at home. She's flying blind.
>
> This information asymmetry — the patient not knowing when to be worried, the doctor not knowing until it's too late — is why **1 in 5 cardiac surgery patients is readmitted within 30 days**. Readmissions that are largely preventable.
>
> CardioCommand fixes this with AI-powered continuous monitoring, intelligent outreach, and a two-sided platform connecting doctors and patients in real time.

---

## The solution — overview (≈45 seconds)

> CardioCommand is a two-sided, AI-native care coordination platform. It has two applications running simultaneously:
>
> **First** — a clinical command center for doctors and nurses, the MD Dashboard — giving them a real-time view of every post-op patient, with AI analysis, automated outreach, and location tracking.
>
> **Second** — a mobile-first patient companion app, where the patient can check their vitals, speak directly to Cora — our AI health assistant — anytime they feel worried, without waiting for a callback.
>
> And everything is connected by a shared intelligence backend that learns from every interaction.
>
> Let me show you both sides.

## Demo — MD Dashboard (≈3 minutes)

### Patient list & risk stratification

> This is the MD Dashboard — the doctor's command center.
>
> On the left, I have a panel of post-cardiac surgery patients. Each card shows the patient's name, surgery type, days post-op, and — critically — a real-time **AI Risk Score** from 0 to 100.
>
> This risk score is computed continuously by our backend using a rule-based clinical model that monitors vital signs, flags deviations from expected recovery trajectories, and weighs factors like days post-op and surgery type. It's not a black box — it's deterministic and auditable, which is critical in a clinical setting.
>
> I can see at a glance which patients need attention today.

### Live vitals monitoring

> When I click on a patient — John Mercer — I get his live vital signs streaming in real time via **WebSockets**.
>
> Heart rate, SpO2, HRV, temperature, blood pressure, respiration rate, steps — all updating every 2 seconds from his wearable device.
>
> These compact trend charts — Heart Rate, SpO2, HRV — give me a quick visual of where he's been in the last few hours. I can see if there's a trend forming, not just a single reading.
>
> Below that, if a vital goes out of range, I get an **Alert Banner** that tells me exactly what's happening and why it's concerning — generated by GPT-4o in plain English, not a code.

### Patient location map

> Now here's something you won't see in most healthcare platforms.
>
> I have a **live patient location map**. Each pin represents a patient. The color tells me their risk level — green is stable, amber is watch, red is urgent.
>
> This matters because post-cardiac patients are often told to gradually increase activity — short walks, light movement. We can see if a patient has wandered too far from home, which could indicate disorientation or a fall risk. We can see if they're not moving at all, which might signal depression or extreme fatigue.
>
> The map updates in real time alongside the vitals stream.

### AI analysis panel — LangGraph pipeline

> Now let me show you the brain of the system.
>
> I click **Analyze Patient**. This triggers our full **LangGraph AI pipeline**.
>
> LangGraph is an orchestration framework for multi-step AI agents. Our pipeline has three nodes:
>
> **Node 1 — RAG retrieval**: We query a FAISS vector store pre-loaded with clinical cardiology guidelines — ACC/AHA post-operative care protocols, medication interaction data, recovery benchmarks. The patient's current vitals and profile are used as the query vector.
>
> **Node 2 — Risk analysis**: A deterministic clinical rule engine evaluates the vitals against thresholds — is the heart rate within expected range for day 8 post-CABG? Is the SpO2 concerning? This produces a structured risk assessment.
>
> **Node 3 — GPT-4o synthesis**: GPT-4o takes the retrieved guidelines, the risk assessment, and the patient's full context — and produces a clinical narrative. Natural language that a doctor can actually read and act on in 30 seconds.
>
> The result: a concise analysis with risk level, specific concerns flagged, and recommended next steps.
>
> I also have one-click **Pre-Visit Brief** — which auto-generates a summary memo for the doctor before a telemedicine call — and **SOAP Note** generation, which writes a structured clinical note ready to paste into the EHR.

### Outreach voice call — Vapi

> But what if I need to proactively reach out to the patient? That's where the **Outreach Script** button comes in.
>
> I click it — and this is our **Vapi-powered voice agent**.
>
> Let me tell you about the tech stack here, because this is something I'm really proud of.
>
> We're using **Vapi.ai** — a platform specifically designed for real-time voice AI. Under the hood, Vapi orchestrates three best-in-class models:
>
> - **Deepgram Nova-2** for Speech-to-Text — it's extremely fast and accurate for medical conversation
> - **ElevenLabs** for Text-to-Speech — high quality, natural voice
> - **GPT-4o** as the conversational LLM — with our custom clinical system prompt loaded with patient context
>
> Cora — our AI agent — calls the patient, greets them by name, knows their surgery type and recovery day, and works through a structured health check: overall wellbeing, pain levels, medication adherence, sleep quality, activity levels.
>
> The conversation sounds natural. It's not a phone tree. It's a real conversation.
>
> After the call ends, the transcript is analyzed by GPT-4o and the results — wellbeing score, flags, severity level, recommended actions — are stored in our database and surfaced right here for the doctor to review.

## Demo — Patient App (≈2 minutes)

> Now let's flip to the patient's perspective.
>
> This is the **Patient App** — designed for someone who is 67 years old, recovering from surgery, and not necessarily tech-savvy. Every design decision was made with that user in mind.
>
> At the top, John gets a warm greeting — it's context-aware, it knows what time of day it is. Below that, a recovery progress bar showing him exactly where he is in his 30-day recovery journey.
>
> **Mood check-in** — because emotional state matters in cardiac recovery. Depression post-surgery is correlated with worse outcomes. If John taps "Scared" or "Not great", it goes into his data.
>
> **Live vitals** — his wearable data, presented in plain English. Not "72 BPM" with no context — it says "In normal range". Not "96%" — it says "Looking good". We translate clinical numbers into reassurance.
>
> **Medications** — a simple checklist of today's medications with times.

### The voice call feature

> Now here's the feature I'm most excited about on the patient side.
>
> You see this green button up here with the microphone icon — and this **"Talk to Cora"** card.
>
> John wakes up at 2 AM with chest tightness. He doesn't know if it's normal healing or something serious. He doesn't want to call the emergency line for nothing. But he's scared.
>
> He taps **Start Voice Call**. Cora picks up instantly.
>
> Unlike the outbound call where Cora follows a structured script, this is an **open-ended inbound call**. Cora's entire role is to listen. She asks open questions: "Tell me more about that." "When did it start?" "On a scale of 1 to 10, how would you describe the pain?"
>
> She's warm, she's empathetic, and she never diagnoses. She always says: "I'm going to make sure your care team sees this right away."
>
> For truly urgent symptoms — chest pain radiating to the arm, severe difficulty breathing — she immediately says: "Please call 911 right now."
>
> When the call ends, the conversation is analyzed, a severity level is assigned, and the doctor's dashboard gets updated — John's doctor may wake up to a notification that her patient called at 2 AM describing chest tightness, severity: moderate, recommended action: morning follow-up call.
>
> That's 24/7 intelligent triage. Without a human operator.

## Architecture (≈90 seconds)

> Let me briefly walk through the architecture.
>
> We built this as a **monorepo** with three main components.
>
> **The backend** is a Python **FastAPI** service. FastAPI gave us async support out of the box, which is critical for real-time WebSocket streaming of vitals data. Every patient has a dedicated WebSocket connection that streams readings every 2 seconds.
>
> The AI pipeline runs on **LangGraph** — which lets us define our multi-step AI reasoning as a directed graph, so each step — retrieval, analysis, synthesis — is modular and testable.
>
> For Retrieval Augmented Generation, we use **FAISS** — Facebook's fast vector similarity search library — as our vector store for clinical guidelines. When we analyze a patient, we don't just ask GPT-4o to guess — we give it the actual clinical evidence relevant to that patient's situation.
>
> Our database is **SQLite via SQLAlchemy** — auto-provisioned on startup, no setup required for the demo. Every voice call, every transcript, every analysis is persisted with a full audit trail.
>
> **The MD Dashboard** and **Patient App** are both **React 18** applications built with **Vite**. We use **Tailwind CSS** for styling, **Framer Motion** for animations, **Recharts** for the vitals charts, and **Leaflet** for the patient location map.
>
> For voice, both apps integrate **Vapi's Web SDK** directly in the browser — no phone number required for the demo. The Vapi assistant configuration — including the system prompt, voice settings, and conversation parameters — is generated dynamically by the backend for each patient, so Cora always knows exactly who she's talking to.
>
> The whole thing runs locally with one backend server and two frontend dev servers.

## Anticipated Q&A

### Is this HIPAA compliant?

> For this demo, we have HIPAA-sensitive features like patient data storage and voice calls flagged — in production, we would implement end-to-end encryption, access controls, and BAAs with all third-party providers including Vapi and OpenAI. The architecture is designed with compliance as a first-class concern — that's why we have a call audit trail in the database.

### How accurate is the AI risk score?

> The risk score uses deterministic clinical rules — it's not a neural network making opaque predictions. It evaluates specific vital sign thresholds against evidence-based guidelines from the ACC/AHA. This means it's auditable and explainable — a doctor can always see exactly why a score was assigned.

### What happens if the patient doesn't have a smartphone?

> Vapi supports outbound phone calls — the doctor can trigger a call to the patient's regular phone number from the dashboard. The same AI agent works over a regular cell call, no app required.

### What's the scalability story?

> The WebSocket architecture handles real-time streaming for multiple patients simultaneously. The LangGraph pipeline is stateless and horizontally scalable. In production, we'd move from SQLite to PostgreSQL and deploy on Kubernetes. The core architecture is already designed for scale.
